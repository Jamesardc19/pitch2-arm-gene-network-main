 CMSC 191 NS | Sub-Project: Pitch 1
Project (Sub-project) Name: ARG Co-occurrence Network Under Climate Gradients: A Gene-Level
 Network Topology Study
Project Members: James Angelo R. Dela Cruz, Angeleen Claire M. Lominoque, Hazelle N. Millarpis
Project Description:
A network science study investigating whether Antibiotic Resistance Genes (ARGs) found co-occurring in
 environmental metagenomic samples form structurally distinct network communities depending on climate
 zone.
Using preprocessed ARG abundance tables sourced from published metagenomic studies on
 Figshare, Zenodo, or NCBI-linked supplementary data, and mapped against Koppen-Geiger climate
 classifications, the project constructs weighted ARG co-occurrence networks and applies community
 detection algorithms to test whether climate zone is a statistically significant predictor of ARG network
 topology.
Project Goals:
1. Source and compile preprocessed ARG abundance tables from published studies with known sample
 geographic coordinates.
2. Assign climate zone labels (tropical, temperate, arid) to each sample using Koppen-Geiger
 classification.
3. Construct weighted ARG co-occurrence networks per climate zone and compute baseline topology
 metrics including degree, modularity, and betweenness centrality.
4. Apply community detection algorithms (Louvain, Girvan-Newman) and compare ARG community
 structures across climate zones to evaluate climate zone as a structural predictor.

4. Issues
- Dataset Availability: Not all published metagenomic studies deposit clean ARG abundance tables
 alongside their papers. Finding a dataset that is both preprocessed and geographically tagged requires
 evaluating multiple candidates, which takes time.
Geographic Metadata: Some datasets report sample locations at the country level rather than exact
 coordinates, which complicates direct Koppen-Geiger zone assignment and may require manual
 classification.

5. Risks
- Insufficient Climate Zone Coverage: Available preprocessed datasets may not have sufficient sample
 coverage across all three target climate zones. If arid zone representation is too sparse, the comparison
 may need to be narrowed to two zones (tropical and temperate).
Mitigation: assess zone distribution before committing to the full three-zone design.
Sparse Networks Per Zone: If co-occurrence networks turn out to be too sparse per climate zone,
 community detection algorithms may not produce meaningful results.
Mitigation: apply a minimum sample threshold per zone before running full analysis.
- Timeline Compression: The 3-week timeline leaves little room for unexpected delays in the data
 preparation phase. If dataset sourcing extends beyond the first week, the depth of analysis may need to
 be reduced to a single community detection algorithm.
Mitigation: prioritize getting one clean, working network over building all three zones simultaneously.
- Writing-Analysis Coordination: There is a risk that the written Methods and Results sections will need
 revision once actual network outputs are generated.
Mitigation: teammates will write the Introduction, RRL, and Background sections first, which do not depend on results, and will hold Methods and Results until after preprocessing is complete.
