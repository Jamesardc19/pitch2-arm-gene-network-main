%% ─── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\abstract{%
Antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) in environmental metagenomes do not
accumulate in isolation; they are co-selected, co-transferred, and
co-regulated in ways that produce structured co-occurrence patterns.
Whether these patterns vary systematically with climate zone remains
largely unexplored.
This study constructs weighted ARG co-occurrence networks for three
major climate zones---tropical, arid, and temperate---using the
mARG dataset (Martiny \emph{et~al.}) comprising 119,232 metagenomic
samples, with climate zone assigned via the Beck \emph{et~al.}\ (2023)
K\"oppen-Geiger raster at $0.1^\circ$ resolution.
Pairwise Spearman correlations between 156 ARGs passing a 5\% presence
filter were used to define edges ($r \geq 0.3$, $p < 0.05$).
Louvain community detection revealed modularity scores of
$Q = 0.41$ (tropical), $Q = 0.26$ (arid), and $Q = 0.31$ (temperate),
indicating zone-specific variation in network compartmentalisation.
Hub ARGs identified by betweenness centrality differed across zones,
with \texttt{tet(M)} recurring in both tropical and arid networks.
A Kruskal-Wallis test on betweenness distributions across zones
yielded $H = 3.22$, $p = 0.20$ (not significant), suggesting that
while the \emph{identity} of hub ARGs differs by zone, the overall
structural centrality distribution is conserved.
These findings provide a network-level perspective on how climate context
may shape the ecology of antibiotic resistance.}

%% ─── 1. Introduction ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Introduction}\label{intro}

The global rise of antibiotic resistance constitutes one of the most
urgent public-health challenges of the twenty-first century.
Antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) are the molecular currency of this
crisis: they encode biochemical mechanisms---enzymatic inactivation,
efflux pumps, target modification---that render antibiotics
ineffective~\cite{WHO2023}.
Environmental metagenomics has demonstrated that ARGs are pervasive
across soils, sediments, and waterways, raising concern about the
``resistome'' as a reservoir from which clinically relevant resistance
elements may emerge~\cite{Allen2010}.

ARGs are rarely found in isolation.
They are frequently co-localised on mobile genetic elements such as
plasmids and integrons, creating co-selection pressure that drives
their co-occurrence in metagenomic samples~\cite{Partridge2018}.
Network science offers a natural framework for studying these
co-occurrence patterns.
By treating ARGs as nodes and statistically significant co-occurrences
as edges, one can characterise the global structure of resistance gene
ecology---identifying hub genes that link distinct resistance clusters,
and detecting communities of genes that tend to co-occur together.

Climate zone is a plausible predictor of ARG community structure.
Temperature and precipitation shape microbial community composition,
horizontal gene transfer rates, and the selective pressure from
antibiotic use in agriculture~\cite{vanBoeckel2014, Cavicchioli2019}.
Yet to our knowledge, no study has directly compared ARG co-occurrence
\emph{network topology} across climate zones using a large,
geographically diverse metagenomic dataset.

This study addresses that gap.
Using the mARG dataset---the largest unified ARG abundance table
compiled from published environmental metagenomic studies---we
assign climate zone labels via the high-resolution
K{\"o}ppen-Geiger raster of Beck \emph{et~al.}\ (2023), construct
weighted co-occurrence networks for tropical, arid, and temperate
zones, and compare their topology through community detection and
centrality analysis.

%% ─── 2. Methods ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Methods}\label{methods}

\subsection{Dataset}\label{sec:data}

We used the mARG dataset published by Martiny \emph{et~al.}\ (Zenodo
accession 6919377), which aggregates ARG abundance tables from 119,232
environmental metagenomic samples.
The dataset provides, for each sample-ARG pair, the fragment count
aligned to a reference ARG sequence (\texttt{fragmentCountAln}),
the reference sequence length, and the total number of trimmed reads
(\texttt{trimmed\_fragments}).
Geographic coordinates were parsed from the accompanying
\texttt{metadata.tsv} file, which records sample location in
degree-minute format (e.g.\ ``18.05 S 47.03 E'').

Abundance was normalised to Reads Per Kilobase per Million mapped reads
(RPKM):
\begin{equation}
  \text{RPKM}_{ij} =
    \frac{\text{fragmentCountAln}_{ij} \times 10^{6}}
         {\left(\text{refLength}_{j} / 1000\right)
          \times \text{trimmedFragments}_{i}}
  \label{eq:rpkm}
\end{equation}
where $i$ indexes samples and $j$ indexes ARG sequences.
This normalisation accounts for both gene length bias and variation in
sequencing depth.
The resulting wide-format matrix has dimensions
$119{,}232 \text{ samples} \times 2{,}999 \text{ ARGs}$.

\subsection{Climate Zone Assignment}\label{sec:zones}

Each sample was assigned to a major climate class using the
Beck \emph{et~al.}\ (2023) K{\"o}ppen-Geiger raster at
$0.1^\circ$ spatial resolution (Figshare dataset 21789074).
The raster encodes 30 climate types as integer codes; we mapped
these to three analytical zones:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Tropical} (codes 1--3, class A)
  \item \textbf{Arid} (codes 4--7, class B)
  \item \textbf{Temperate} (codes 8--28, classes C and D merged)
  \item Polar (codes 29--30, class E) --- excluded
\end{itemize}
Point queries were performed in Python using \texttt{rasterio}.
Samples with coordinates outside the raster extent or returning a
no-data value were discarded.
This yielded 52,064 samples across the three zones
(Table~\ref{tab:zones}).

\subsection{ARG Filtering}\label{sec:filter}

ARGs present in fewer than 5\% of samples (i.e.\ fewer than
$0.05 \times 52{,}064 \approx 2{,}603$ samples) were removed as
insufficiently prevalent to construct meaningful co-occurrence edges.
This reduced the feature set from 2,999 to \textbf{156 ARGs}.

\subsection{Co-occurrence Network Construction}\label{sec:network}

For each climate zone, we computed all pairwise Spearman rank
correlations between the 156 retained ARG abundance vectors.
An undirected, weighted edge was added between ARG $i$ and ARG $j$
if and only if:
\begin{equation}
  r_s(i, j) \geq 0.3 \quad \text{and} \quad p < 0.05
  \label{eq:edge}
\end{equation}
where $r_s$ is the Spearman correlation coefficient and $p$ is its
two-tailed $p$-value.
The threshold $r \geq 0.3$ follows established practice in
microbiome co-occurrence network studies~\cite{Faust2012} and
excludes weakly correlated pairs that may reflect chance associations.
Only positive correlations were retained, as negative correlations
are ecologically ambiguous in this context (competitive exclusion
\emph{vs.} differential habitat preference).
Networks were implemented as weighted undirected graphs in Python
using \texttt{NetworkX} and exported as \texttt{.gexf} files.

\subsection{Community Detection}\label{sec:community}

Community structure was assessed using two algorithms:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Louvain algorithm} (primary)~\cite{Blondel2008}:
    a greedy modularity-maximisation heuristic that iteratively
    reassigns nodes to communities to improve the modularity score
    $Q$, defined as
    \begin{equation}
      Q = \frac{1}{2m} \sum_{ij}
          \left[ A_{ij} - \frac{k_i k_j}{2m} \right]
          \delta(c_i, c_j)
      \label{eq:modularity}
    \end{equation}
    where $A_{ij}$ is the edge weight, $k_i$ is the weighted degree
    of node $i$, $m$ is the total edge weight, $c_i$ is the community
    assignment of node $i$, and $\delta$ is the Kronecker delta.
    Values of $Q > 0.3$ are conventionally regarded as indicating
    meaningful community structure~\cite{Newman2006}.
  \item \textbf{Girvan-Newman algorithm} (comparison)~\cite{Girvan2002}:
    a divisive hierarchical approach that removes edges with the
    highest betweenness centrality iteratively; the first-level
    partition was used.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Centrality Metrics}\label{sec:centrality}

Three node-level centrality measures were computed per zone:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Degree centrality}: fraction of other nodes to which
    a given ARG is connected.
  \item \textbf{Betweenness centrality}: proportion of shortest paths
    in the network that pass through a given ARG, normalised by the
    total number of pairs.
    Hub ARGs with high betweenness act as bridges between co-occurrence
    communities.
  \item \textbf{Clustering coefficient}: probability that two
    neighbours of a given ARG are themselves connected,
    reflecting local clustering.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Cross-zone Comparison}\label{sec:stats}

To test whether betweenness centrality distributions differ
significantly across climate zones, we applied the
Kruskal-Wallis $H$-test~\cite{Kruskal1952}, a non-parametric
one-way analysis of variance appropriate for non-normal distributions.
Significance was set at $\alpha = 0.05$.

Louvain community detection results are presented in

co-occurrence network.

\subsection{Hub ARGs by Betweenness Centrality}\label{sec:res-hubs}

\subsection{Cross-zone Centrality Comparison}\label{sec:res-kw}

The Kruskal-Wallis test on betweenness centrality distributions
across the three zones yielded $H = 3.219$, $p = 0.200$
(Figure~\ref{fig:boxplot}).
This result is not statistically significant at $\alpha = 0.05$,
indicating that the \emph{distribution} of betweenness centrality
values is statistically similar across zones.
The apparent zone differences in hub ARG identity (Section~\ref{sec:res-hubs})
therefore reflect shifts in \emph{which} ARGs occupy central positions,
rather than a change in the overall degree of network centralisation.

\section{Discussion}\label{discussion}

\subsection{Climate-stratified ARG network structure}

All three co-occurrence networks exhibit properties consistent with
other biological co-occurrence networks: sparse but non-trivial density,
high clustering coefficients, and modular organisation.
The zone-level differences in modularity are particularly informative.
The tropical network's high modularity ($Q = 0.41$) may reflect the
compartmentalisation of ARG co-occurrence within distinct ecological
niches---such as soil, freshwater, and marine---each exerting different
selective pressures on microbial communities.
The comparatively low modularity of the arid zone ($Q = 0.26$) could
indicate greater integration of resistance genes across sample types,
perhaps driven by the harsher, more uniform environmental filter of
arid climates, or by a more homogeneous microbial pool.

\subsection{Hub ARG identity across zones}

The recurrence of \texttt{tet(M)} as a top hub in both tropical and
arid zones is consistent with its well-documented mobility on
conjugative transposons (Tn916 family), which facilitate its spread
across phylogenetically diverse bacterial hosts~\cite{Roberts2011}.
Its structural centrality in the network suggests that it serves as a
connector between distinct resistance clusters, which is ecologically
consistent with its role as one of the most widely disseminated
tetracycline-resistance determinants.

The dominance of \texttt{lnu(B)} and \texttt{aph(6)-Id} in the
temperate hub profile, genes associated with agricultural and
clinical residues, is broadly consistent with the heavier antibiotic
use in the temperate-zone countries (primarily North America and
Europe) represented in the mARG dataset~\cite{vanBoeckel2014}.

\texttt{lsa(E)}, a pleuromutilin-lincosamide-streptogramin resistance
gene originally identified in \emph{Enterococcus}~\cite{Lozano2012},
appeared as a hub in both arid and temperate zones.
This cross-zone prevalence may reflect horizontal dissemination on
broad-host-range plasmids independent of climate context.

\subsection{Interpretation of non-significant Kruskal-Wallis result}

The non-significant Kruskal-Wallis test ($p = 0.20$) should not be
interpreted as evidence that climate zone has no effect on ARG
network structure.
Network-level comparison via overall centrality distribution is a
coarse measure; it cannot capture the zone-specific shifts in hub ARG
identity documented in Table~\ref{tab:hubs}.
Moreover, the heavy overrepresentation of temperate samples
(85.7\% of the zoned dataset) may have suppressed inter-zone variation
by biasing the temperate distribution with very high statistical power
relative to tropical and arid.
Future work with more balanced zone sampling would provide a more
definitive test.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations bear noting.
First, the dataset is heavily skewed toward temperate-zone samples;
tropical and arid zones each contribute fewer than 10\% of the zoned
data, which limits the statistical power of inter-zone comparisons.
Second, the 5\% presence filter, while necessary for network
stability, excluded 94.8\% of ARGs, potentially omitting
zone-specific rare resistance elements.
Third, the Spearman correlation threshold ($r \geq 0.3$) and
$p$-value cutoff ($< 0.05$) are conventional but arbitrary; network
topology is sensitive to these choices.
Finally, co-occurrence does not imply physical co-localisation on
a mobile genetic element; confounding by shared ecological niche
cannot be excluded without confirmatory genomic evidence.

%% ─── 5. Conclusion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\section{Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

This study presents the first large-scale, climate-stratified
co-occurrence network analysis of environmental ARGs.
Using the mARG dataset and K{\"o}ppen-Geiger climate zone assignment,
we constructed weighted ARG co-occurrence networks for tropical, arid,
and temperate zones and characterised their topology through community
detection and centrality analysis.

Key findings are: (1) all three zones exhibit non-trivial modular
structure; (2) tropical networks are the most compartmentalised
($Q = 0.41$), while arid networks are the most integrated
($Q = 0.26$); (3) \texttt{tet(M)} and \texttt{lsa(E)} recur as
structural hubs across multiple zones, consistent with their known
mobility; and (4) overall betweenness centrality distributions are
statistically similar across zones despite qualitative differences
in hub ARG identity.

These findings contribute to our understanding of how climate context
shapes the ecology of antibiotic resistance at the gene-network level
and provide a framework for future comparative resistome studies with
more geographically balanced sampling.
